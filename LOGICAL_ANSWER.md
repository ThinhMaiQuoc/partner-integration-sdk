## Q7 - Best seat on a bus

### Answer

My default choice is a right-side window seat, around the middle of the bus or a bit behind it. Not the front priority seats, not the last row, and not a seat that blocks people moving in or out.

This is my default only when the bus is not too crowded and nobody clearly needs the seat more than me. If there is an elderly person, a pregnant woman, a parent with a child, someone injured, or someone carrying heavy things, I would give up the seat. A good seat is not good if it creates a problem for someone else.

### Why

I think about the bad cases first. In Vietnam traffic, the side closer to heavier moving traffic and lane changes feels more exposed. A right-side window seat is usually closer to the curb side, so I would feel slightly better protected from side collision risk compared with sitting on the left side. It is not a guarantee, but it is a small defensive choice.

The window also matters in a serious emergency. If the aisle is blocked, the door is jammed, or there is smoke or fire, being close to a window may give one more possible exit. I am not saying I want to break glass as a normal plan. I just do not like having only one escape path if the situation goes bad.

Middle seats are also better than the very back for stability. The last row can bounce more and is slower to leave from. The front is useful for people who need quick access and easier movement.

### Trade-off

For a short trip, I may stand near the door instead, because getting off fast matters more than sitting. For a long trip, I care more about stability, space, and not being trapped. So my answer is simple: right-side window near the middle is my default, but I change it if the bus is crowded, if the trip is short, or if someone else needs the seat more.

## Q12 - Fastest way to refuel

### Answer

I assume a motorbike at a normal gas station in Vietnam. The fastest way is: choose the right pump early, stop with the fuel cap facing the staff, turn off the engine, open the cap, say the fuel type and amount clearly, prepare payment before pumping finishes, close the cap, and leave the pump area immediately.

### Process

First, I would not blindly choose the shortest line. I would choose the line that is moving. A short line can still be slow if someone is asking for an invoice, arguing about payment, finding change, or parked at the wrong angle.

Before reaching the pump, I should already know what I want: type of fuel, fixed amount, or full tank. When I stop, the bike should be positioned so the staff does not need to stretch the hose or ask me to move. I should turn off the engine, avoid phone use unless it is needed for QR payment, and keep the bike stable.

Payment should be ready before the fuel is done. Exact cash is fastest. QR is fine if the station supports it and the network is stable. If I know QR may fail, I should still have cash ready. After payment, I should move away first, then arrange my wallet, gloves, or phone somewhere else.

### Why

The bottleneck is rarely the fuel going into the tank. The slow parts are bad lane choice, bad positioning, unclear communication, and payment delay. If I remove those, the whole stop becomes faster without acting rushed.

### Trade-off

The worst case at a gas station is not losing ten seconds. It is fuel spill, fire, someone hitting the bike, or being trapped between vehicles. So I would never trade safety for speed: engine off, no careless phone use, no sudden cutting across lanes, and leave myself enough room to move if something goes wrong.

## Q14 - Best entrance door for a public men's restroom

### Answer

My first choice is no door: a Z-shaped or maze-style entrance. People can walk in and out without touching anything, while the wall still blocks the view from outside.

If there is not enough space, I would use a simple outward push door with a good closer, plus a foot pull or arm pull for exiting. I would keep it mechanical and low-tech unless the building really needs something more expensive.

### Constraints

The entrance has to solve hygiene, privacy, traffic flow, smell, accessibility, maintenance, cost, and emergency access. If it solves only one of those and creates problems in the others, it is not a good design.

### Options

A normal pull/push door is cheap, but the handle is the weak point. After washing hands, people still need to touch the same handle used by everyone else. It also becomes a choke point when people enter and leave at the same time.

An automatic sliding door sounds clean, but it relies on sensors, power, and maintenance. If it fails, it becomes worse than a normal door. It can also be too slow or annoying in a busy public place.

A push-only door with a foot pull is better. It is simple and reduces hand contact. The issue is that it is still a physical door. It can be blocked, slammed, damaged, or become a traffic pinch point.

The Z-shaped entrance is best if space allows. No handle, no sensor, no door closer, no lock, fewer failure points. It protects privacy by layout instead of hardware.

### Trade-off

The trade-off is space. A Z entrance needs more floor area and must still work for wheelchairs, cleaning carts, and emergency help. If the space is too small, forcing a maze would be bad design. So my answer is: use the no-door Z entrance in malls, airports, and large offices; use a simple low-touch outward door in smaller restrooms. I would avoid fancy parts unless they clearly reduce risk.

## Q15 - Choose a fruit stall quickly

### Answer

I would choose the stall with the most transparent selling setup, not the prettiest fruit. My quick signal is: clear prices, a scale facing the customer, old but well-maintained tools, and signs that the stall serves both small buyers and bigger regular buyers.

### Signal

I would look for one stall where the transaction looks hard to manipulate. The scale should be visible. Prices should not be hidden. Bags, baskets, and crates should show that the stall handles different buying sizes. The tools can be old, but they should look cared for. The produce should be arranged for selling, not only for display.

If the scale is hidden behind the seller's side, if the prices are missing, or if only the top layer of fruit looks good, I would treat that as a warning.

### Why

At lunchtime, I cannot rely on a crowd. The seller may be resting, so I cannot ask questions. I also should not pretend I can fully judge "good, nutritious, and cheap" just by fruit color. Fruit can be polished, stacked, or rotated so the best pieces are on top.

The stall setup tells me about the seller's habits. A visible scale means less room for cheating. Clear prices mean less room for random pricing. Tools that look used but maintained suggest the stall has been running for a while. Mixed packaging suggests repeat customers, not only one-time buyers.

### Trade-off

This does not guarantee the sweetest fruit. It just lowers the chance of choosing a stall with hidden tricks. In the worst case, I may still get average fruit, but I am less likely to get cheated on weight, price, or selection. So I would choose the stall with the clearest process, because a fair process is a better quick signal than a nice display.

## Q17 - Guiding a child in familiar vs unfamiliar places

### Answer

In a familiar place, I would let the child lead more. In an unfamiliar place, I would lead more. I would not use one fixed rule, because the child's behavior changes with confidence.

The point is not whether I use hand-holding, carrying, or letting the child walk alone. Those are just tools. The real question is whether the child is showing that they want to lead or want to be led.

### Why

In a familiar place, the child may walk ahead, point to the next turn, choose the route, pull away from the hand, or act like they already know the map. If the area is reasonably safe, I would let them lead inside a boundary. I still watch roads, stairs, elevators, doors, and strangers. But I do not need to control every step.

In an unfamiliar place, the child usually gives different signals. They may stay close, hold tighter, look back, slow down, or stop at turns. In that case, making them "be independent" can be the wrong move. They are already saying they do not understand the place. I should lead clearly, keep them on the safer side, and make fewer open-ended choices.

I also think about the worst case. In a crowded mall, traffic area, station, or parking lot, a small delay can become dangerous. If the child hesitates or runs the wrong way, reaction time matters. So in unfamiliar or high-risk places, I would take control earlier.

### Trade-off

The trade-off is freedom versus recovery time. In familiar places, I can give more freedom because I can recover from small mistakes. In unfamiliar places, I reduce freedom because a mistake can become harder to fix. Adults are similar. People want autonomy where they feel strong, and guidance where they feel uncertain. The parent should read the signal and adjust, not force one style everywhere.
